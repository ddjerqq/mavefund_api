January 4, 2023

The old year is barely digested, and the new one is here. What will 2023 bring? As uncertain and as yet unwritten the future is at least certain: after a difficult twelve months in 2022, investors are hoping for improvement this year. Alone: ​​Hope is not always the best advisor. A look at possible surprises.

“Predictions are extremely difficult, especially when they concern the future,” the great American writer Mark Twain used to say. It's no different for analysts, market experts and journalists when they look into the crystal ball at the beginning of the year.

In the financial world, the renowned investment strategist Byron Wien created a new forecasting standard in the 1980s with his “ 10 Surprises ”: events that, according to today’s consensus, would be a surprise but have a certain probability of occurrence. In that tradition, and with food for thought that is, of course, not investment advice, I venture a look at five possible events that could happen this year that have the potential to make a mark on the financial world.

1. Inflation and policy rates are holding up more stubbornly than expected

The big all-clear is missing. Inflation is here to stay - especially in Europe, where the ECB continues to tighten interest rates more than hoped. Towards the end of the year, the key interest rate approaches 4 percent, while inflation remains at 5 percent. In the USA, the strong medicine of the Fed seems to take effect more quickly, but it also does not get the inflation rate below four percent in the long term - accordingly, possible interest rate cuts are postponed to 2024. This is not least due to the robust economy, which is not showing any sign of recession slip away - at least on the other side of the Atlantic. The situation is different in this country: the German economy will shrink in 2023.

2. Nothing new in the East: The Ukraine war is not ending

It is the drama of the 21st century: the Ukraine war, which will be celebrated for the first time next month. A military standoff paralyzes the region and the rest of the world. Ukraine continues to fight heroically, pushing Russia back further – but the nuclear power Russia's sword of Damocles hangs over the hope of an end.

In addition, the situation in the Kremlin seems unpredictable: would Putin accept a withdrawal and survive politically? And if not, what comes next? The situation in Ukraine remains correspondingly confused and a permanent burden for Europe. The Federal Republic in particular needs time to free itself from its dependence on Russia for its energy supply. Over the course of the year, the persistently high oil, gas and electricity prices increasingly burden citizens and the economy - Germany remains the problem child of the EU.

3. The recovery on the world stock exchanges comes, but then ebbs away again - gold, on the other hand, is bullish

On the capital markets, things are once again going differently than you think. Contrary to the very bearish mood at the beginning of the year, a moderate rally developed on Wall Street and the world stock exchanges in the first half of the year. But in the middle of the year, the long-awaited comeback loses its momentum again - neither in the S&P nor in the Dax are there new highs to report in 2023.

Instead, investors are losing their patience with last year's favorites. Defensive stocks have protected investors from worse in 2022, but are long-term P/E ratios north of 20 in consumer stocks and industrials justifiable on marginal growth? In the second half of the year, when it dawns on investors that the 2020s could be a thoroughly dodgy decade, they sell off value stocks that are not cheap at all.

The Dow and Dax are down again for the year as emerging markets stocks outperform established markets after decades of underperformance. Investors, meanwhile, are taking refuge in gold, which after a decade of sideways movement is finally dynamically breaking out and making new highs beyond $2500. Despite falling oil prices, energy stocks are still gaining momentum as most commodity prices are pushed higher by the Ukraine war-related supply shortages.

4. The GAFAMs fail to make a furious comeback

Will the big losers of 2022 be the winners of 2023? no Big Tech has first in the face of far from cheap valuations, flagging growth and a badly battered chart technique continue to have a difficult time . The GAFAM stocks Apple, Microsoft, Amazon & Co. are initially coming under pressure again on Wall Street. (Apple in particular has a lot to lose after years of constant growth of its bestseller iPhone.) At some point, however, enough is enough - the capitulation of the former market darlings finally drives away the young generation of easy money investors.

In the second half of the year, a sector rotation sets in – this time in favor of big tech, which suddenly becomes the new value stocks. The cost-cutting measures are having an effect, the GAFAMs are able to report higher profits again despite stagnating growth. Towards the end of the year, Big Tech is finally able to embark on a sizeable rally, but for the most part it's only good for reversing earlier losses or posting modest gains. Microsoft overtakes Apple as the most valuable tech company, but has to give way to Saudi Aramco in the global stock market ranking.

5. Bitcoin crashes towards $5000

Only a side note is the crypto industry, which was once given so much advance praise. The FTX bankruptcy of 2022 will not have been the last - investors realize over the course of the year, as Bitcoin continues to fall behind, that this crypto winter will last longer, that there may not be a spring in this cycle, that the cycle theory is outdated - and the highly speculative one asset class is past its prime.

As Bitcoin owners watched as the psychologically important $10,000 mark failed to hold, a new wave of panicked sell-offs towards $5,000 began. Long-time advocates have to liquidate their positions, the crypto scene sinks into an apocalyptic mood, following the example of tulip mania in the 17th century. The big promise of the new decentralized money has not been fulfilled.

Disclaimer: These are only food for thought, no forecasts and certainly no investment recommendations.
